Sloan Fellow is a very unique program. It's in a league of its own with
no other benchmark in this business. It's not an MBA, neither an EMBA.
It's a 12-month residence program that grants you a Master of Science
(in management) degree. Its tuition fee is the same as the two-year MBA
program, and its academic rigor is much, much higher than any other EMBA
or executive training program. For experienced professionals around my
age group, Stanford's Sloan Fellow Program is the best and ideal
destination before turning page over to the next chapter.

A comparison between Stanford MBA and Stanford Sloan Fellow.

[![image0](http://www.guizishanren.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/MBAvsSloan.png)](http://www.guizishanren.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/MBAvsSloan.png)

**Age**: we're no longer young or naive...many are bringing their
families to live on campus

**Number in Class**: Sloan's small class size makes it very conducive to
make friends. When graduate school's class size gets over 100, it
becomes difficult just to remember people's names. With our current size
of 81, the distance between any two students is still close enough to
form  meaningful friendship.

**Years of Work Experience**: Many upper-middle managers from large
corporations, some C-level executives from smaller companies, some
senior officials from governments (Singtel's [Lee Hsien
Yang](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Hsien_Yang) is a Sloan Fellow
alumni).

**Relationship within Class**: MBAs are natural competitors within the
class because most of them are shooting for the same jobs: PE firms,
strategy consulting firms, or investment banks. Many might have
different ideas before they come to graduate schools, but the business
model of graduate schools is built to supply new recruits for those
industries, so there is little they can do to escape from that fate. MBA
students' golden window only arrives after they have secured job offers
roughly half a year before their graduation. That's when people really
start relaxing and having a good time without any peer pressure. For
Sloans it's a very different situation. Many self-funded Sloans in my
class want to do startup and are looking for partners. We're natural
partners in that sense. It's a very good atmosphere.

**Value of the Degree**: MBA students do need the degree to prove their
intelligence and drive, that they meet the bar for VC/Consulting/Ibanks.
Sloans don't need this degree to prove they've got what it takes. Many
of my classmates are already overachieving executives or highly
successful entrepreneurs. They're exactly what MBAs ten years younger
aspire to be. A lot of Sloans need this platform as a transition, to
higher leadership positions in governments or global corporations, or to
entirely new areas of interest.

**Change of Career**: mostly yes, for both MBAs and Sloans.

**Attitude on Grades**: MBAs fight for grade because their future
employers use that as an effective filtering tool. Stanford GSB does not
disclose grades to any other party, including other students in the
class. Every Sloan only knows his/her own grades. Therefore, a rational
Sloan would not pay too much attention to grade (unless he/she is
sponsored and his/her sponsor has specific grade requirement). Sloans
pursue grade to the extend that the result satisfies his/her own ego and
that's it.  We know that given our age, it's highly unlikely we'll ever
come back to a university classroom. So we soak up knowledge like a
sponge.

**Source of Pressure**: For MBAs, it's all about finding sexy jobs that
every other smart ass in the class wants to get. For the self-funded
Sloans, the pressure comes mainly from the anxiety of taking startup
ideas off the ground and seeking suitable partners. 12 months is a
transition period, and time really flies before we know it. For
sponsored Sloans, they're all elite A-players from their sponsoring
governments/companies, and their futures are already carefully planned.
Their priorities.. I don't know... maybe get tanned?
