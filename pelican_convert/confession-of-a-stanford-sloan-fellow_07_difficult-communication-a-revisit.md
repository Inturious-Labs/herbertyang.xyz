Today we had a 4-hour session on **How To Make Difficult and Productive
Conversations**, taught and facilitated by Bonnie and Nirit from [Shelly
Anderson & Lanz Lowen](www.wentworthconsulting.com). The milage of this
session varies depending on Sloans' background and level of experience.
For me this is an opportunity to consolidate some of my reflections in
this area after going through gruesome training in the best management
trainee program in General Electric and grinding my last four years in
the startup world in Beijing.

It must have been ten years since I saw this chart last time in GE's FMP
training ([Financial Management Training
Program](http://www.ge.com/careers/students/fmp/index.html)). This chart
is useful for large corporations (and I think for scientific/academic
research too) where the concept of teamwork and collaboration exists.
However it falls short in explaining the complexities or the dynamics of
managing external relationship or the startup world. Since none of my
real-life situations can be captured in the TKI Framework,  I had to
develop the following chart myself:

**HKY Framework For Communication Style**

[![image1](http://www.guizishanren.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/HKY-Framework.png)](http://www.guizishanren.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/HKY-Framework.png)

-   **Directing**: when I work with my subordinates, I have high level
    of authority and I'm result-oriented, so I give orders most of the
    time.
-   **Compromising**: when I work with the other co-founders of my
    company, we all have considerable influence over each other as major
    shareholders. My level of authority is relatively-speaking low in
    this context and I'm still result-oriented (to maximize we
    shareholders' value), so I compromise on most of the issues to
    strike the balance within the group/partnership.
-   **Negotiating**: when I negotiate contracts/projects with other
    companies/organizations, my level of authority can be anywhere in
    the middle - I can never exert absolute power over my counterparts
    but I might have more chips than they do; my goal is two-faced as
    well, I want to close the deal (result-oriented) but I don't want to
    hurt the relationship either and sometime relationship is even more
    important than cutting a deal per se.
-   **Pleasing**: when I work with my board of directors/CEO, my level
    of authority is low, and I value relationship more than result
    because a lot of the interactions with those larger-than-life
    personalities don't lead to any specific business action, but they
    help build the perception/image of me in their minds. Basically I'm
    trying to please them in whichever way I can.
-   **Socializing**: when I work with my suppliers
    (lawyers/accountants/bankers), I have high level of authority
    (because I'm their client, at least a potential one) and I have
    every incentive to transform those platform-specific relationships
    into my personal relationships. A lot of times, we are just talking
    and chilling out together. In other words, we're socializing.(it
    moves downward to negotiating when we're trying to sign a service
    contract, and further downward to directing when we're doing
    projects together)

**Perception Is Reality**

-   Unfortunately, this is true in every sense. People's behavior,
    attitude and judgement on us are based on the perceived image of us
    in their minds. So when we're talking about living in an interactive
    environment, whether being social or business, the perception people
    have on us is really the one that matters. In 2006-2007 during my
    last days in GE I thought about building a website that can capture
    friends' perception on you on the basis of anonymity and reciprocity
    (a more trendy phrase is social graph). I didn't try hard enough
    with that idea because I couldn't appreciate the power of
    [Haskell](http://www.haskell.org/haskellwiki/Haskell) and didn't
    know a kid in Harvard was applying similar idea to ranking the
    hotness of girls on campus.

**Giving Feedback Is A Gift**

-   I can't recall many situations where people are thrilled by the 360
    feedback they receive, and I've done a lot of 360 reviews in GE.
    Usually you already know the glowing reviews people have for you
    (unless you live in a vacuum cage like Magneto), way before those
    comments get into formal 360 process. So, be definition, most of the
    "surprises" coming out of 360 reviews are not the ones you'd expect,
    and to a much lesser extend, enjoy.
-   Putting aside our general dislike of those 360 review surprise
    comments, someone's action of giving feedback to you is ALWAYS a
    gift, because he/she doesn't have to do that (he/she could have just
    given you a middle score with no comment to stay under the radar
    screen) and by doing this (giving feedback) he/she is running the
    risk of severing his/her relationship with you (because there is a
    pretty good chance that you can guess the source of the comment). It
    takes some courage to give feedback to someone you know (assuming
    people do that in a responsible fashion).
-   Therefore the first thing you should do after receiving feedback is
    to thank that person. You may not be friends any more but you should
    still thank him/her.

**The Most Useful Feedback Is The One That You Never Receive**

-   The value of feedback varies from mild surprise to touchy-feely to
    totally useless. The most useful feedback is nonetheless the one you
    will NEVER receive, such as, we like you a lot, but you're just not
    smart enough for this job; you smell badly; you're competent, but a
    total jerk, and no one in the office likes you; you just look too
    young for this executive position; you are our best employee, but
    you have zero charisma and nobody respects you. Everybody knows this
    is the one and only feedback we want to give to that person in the
    team, but nobody dares to say it out loud.
-   I had the pleasure of doing something to the above effect once to
    one of the co-founders of our start-up company in Beijing. It is the
    most difficult conversation I've ever had.
-   That's why sometimes your enemy from the other side of the fence can
    actually help you the most because he/she might just deliver the
    most valuable feedback to you , not your consensus-building
    colleagues from the same department or the same company.

**Beauty Pageant Winner Does NOT Win Every Contest**

-   [![image2](http://www.guizishanren.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/crowdpleasing.png)](http://www.guizishanren.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/crowdpleasing.png)
-   Beauty pageant winners don't win all the contests in real-life. In
    fact, I can only think of one situation: voters-elected government
    legislator/official such as Senator/Congressman/President. Getting
    ahead in corporate ladder or in business in general takes much more
    than just winning the popularity vote.
-   Being popular and liked by many is useful for JUNIOR employees in
    the company because they're vulnerable to too many people with more
    seniority. As one ascends the corporate hierarchy, delivering
    desired result becomes increasingly more important than being the
    best friend for the majority. At CEO level, the measurement of
    success is simple: maximize shareholders' value. Popularity is
    seldom a consideration at that level.

**Chinese Culture and Giving Feedback**

-   Giving feedback in Chinese culture is especially difficult because
    Chinese value face way more than anything else. One thing I really
    like about American culture (at least from TV/movie) is that friends
    can beat the shit out of each other over certain things but after
    that friends can still be friends (let me know if Hollywood
    aggrandizes this as I'm a hopeless junkie in this area). With
    Chinese, you just can never do that. So if a Chinese asks feedback
    from you, be afraid, be very afraid.

**Really Bad Feedback That Nobody Wants To Hear**

    nothing)
-   your critical-thinking is great (so.. you can't execute or do
    operation)
-   you're such a nice guy (you got no memorable personality)
