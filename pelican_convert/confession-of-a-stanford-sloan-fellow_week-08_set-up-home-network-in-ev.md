Setting up my home network is not as easy as it first looks. It took me
quite a while to figure this out. I'm using all Macs - iMac, iPad, and
iPhone. Here's how to set up the eventual air-play and home-sharing
(both features are only available to Mac users) on Apple TV, which
projects everything on TV.

​1. **Register your every networking device** at Stanford Residential
Computing's page: http://rescomp.stanford.edu, including your router,
iMac, iPad, iPhone, PS3, and Apple TV.  All you need for the
registration is the MAC (Media Access Control) address for your device,
which is also called wi-fi address. It follows this format:
XX:XX:XX:XX:XX:XX. There is one unique MAC address for each device and
it can always be found either on the device itself or in its settings
(it's like a VIN number for your car's engine). When you register for
devices such as iMac, iPad, iPhone, you can work through the
registration process on that device itself, because they provide a user
interface/browser for you to do that. When you register for PS3, router
or Apple TV, you have to do that on ANOTHER device (like iMac/Macbook)
because PS3/router/Apple TV do not have an interface themselves. One of
the questions during that rescomp registration process goes something
like " are you registering for the current device you're using?" . Check
"No" when you're doing this for router/Apple TV.

​2. **Get a router**. Can you still get online without a router? Yes of
course. You can always just connect your device to "Stanford Residence",
but it's advisable to get a router for your apartment home because you
can only get airply and home-sharing to work if you have a router.
 Router is almost a commodity and is cheap. You can get a decent one
under \$100 from Fry's. Register your router as well at rescomp page.

​3. Once the router is configured, you can now **set up a home wi-fi
network from your router**. Usually you go to an address like
192.168.1.X to do that. Google "default IP address for XXX" (substitute
XXX for the brand/model of your router) to get that IP address for the
admin page of the router. Set a password for your home wi-fi network.
This set up is not the most common or intuitive one. Usually your router
is connected to a wall-jacket by a network cable to some broadband/cable
connection provided by the local carrier, then it creates a wi-fi
network and all your devices are connected to the wi-fi network. In
Stanford, you can also connect to "Stanford Residences" through a
wall-jacket, but there is really no need to do that. So for the
residents living in Escondido Village, your home network is sort of
wireless-to-wireless, instead of cable-to-wireless. Most if not all the
routers in the market should be able to support the wireless-to-wireless
configuration.

​4. If you use the **idiosyncratic Time Capsule** like me, you're in for
a ride at this point. Time Capsule is unique and it is quite a challenge
to set up its network and connect that to "Stanford Residencess",
because Apple (being Apple) has very restrictive settings for Time
Capsule that is very different from other "normal" routers in the
market. Contact me if you run into problems with Time Capsule. I'm not
at liberty to go into more details about Time Capsule openly.

​5. Connect your every device including Apple TV to the newly created
home wi-fi network. Getting online should be no problem now (well, it is
never a problem even without the router).

6.  **Connect your Apple TV to your TV** with an HDMI cable.

​7. Under the same home wi-fi network, **Airplay** will work now between
your iOS device (iPad/iPhone/Macbook Air/iMac) and Apple TV, and Apple
TV will project everything on your device to the TV screen. If you don't
set up a home wi-fi network and only connect to "Stanford Residences",
airplay WILL NOT work because Apple TV cannot see your iOS device on the
massive "Stanford Residences" network.

​8. By the same token, your **Home-Sharing from the iPhoto/iTunes** on
your iMac will also work like a charm now, which is really cool. I can
now flip through all my photo albums on the 47-inch TV and use its
stereo system to play my numerous playlists from my iTunes.

​9. Another reason why you should get a router and set up a home wi-fi
network - **privacy** with Airplay and Home-sharing. If you only connect
to Stanford Residences and are not careful about Airplay/Home-sharing,
you might allow unintended parties to peek into your personal stuff.
